UNESCO World Heritage
Mountain Shrines · Tochigi Prefecture

Nikko

日光 · Sunlight

Nikko sits in the forested mountains of Tochigi Prefecture, two hours north of Tokyo by train, and contains some of the most ornate religious architecture in Japan. The Toshogu Shrine complex — built in the 17th century as a mausoleum for Tokugawa Ieyasu, the shogun who unified Japan — is a riot of gilded carvings, lacquered gates, and painted panels set beneath towering cedar trees that have stood for four centuries. Beyond the shrine complex, Nikko National Park offers waterfalls, mountain lakes, hot springs, and a stretch of the old Edo-period highway lined with ancient cryptomeria cedars that is one of the most atmospheric walks in Japan.

💡 Terry recommends arriving early — the Toshogu inner sanctum fills up by mid-morning. The Shinkansen to Utsunomiya followed by the Nikko Limited Express is faster and more comfortable than the direct Tobu Nikko Line from Asakusa. Don't miss the Shinkyo sacred bridge and the quieter Futarasan Shrine just beyond the crowds.

National Treasure Castle
Castle Town · Nagano Prefecture

Matsumoto

松本 · Pine Origin

Matsumoto is a beautifully preserved castle town in the Japan Alps, two and a half hours west of Tokyo on the Azusa Limited Express from Shinjuku. Its centrepiece is Matsumoto Castle — nicknamed the Crow Castle for its dramatic black exterior — one of only twelve original castles remaining in Japan and a National Treasure. The castle sits at the edge of the old town, reflected in the moat, with the snow-capped Japanese Alps rising behind it. The surrounding streets of the Nakamachi and Nawate districts are lined with Edo-period kura storehouses, sake breweries, and craft shops that give Matsumoto a character unlike anywhere closer to Tokyo.

💡 The castle interior is open to climb — the wooden staircases are steep and original, giving a genuine sense of the building's age. Terry recommends pairing Matsumoto with an overnight stay and a morning visit to the Daio Wasabi Farm nearby — one of Japan's largest wasabi plantations, set along a clear mountain river and extraordinarily photogenic.

Coastal Escape
Peninsula · Shizuoka Prefecture

Izu Peninsula

伊豆半島 · Hot Spring Coast

The Izu Peninsula juts into the Pacific south of Tokyo and is one of the most scenically varied day trip destinations in the region — a landscape of dramatic sea cliffs, hidden coves, bamboo forests, geothermal hot springs, and quiet fishing villages strung along a coastline that faces both the open Pacific and the calmer waters of Suruga Bay, with Mount Fuji visible from the west coast on clear days. The peninsula has been a retreat for artists, writers, and the Tokyo wealthy since the Meiji era, and its towns — Atami, Ito, Shuzenji, Shimoda — each have a distinct character ranging from the onsen resort bustle of Atami to the quiet beauty of Shuzenji's temple valley.

💡 Terry's favourite Izu route takes you to Shuzenji — a small temple town set in a bamboo-lined valley around a hot spring that has flowed since the 9th century — followed by the coastal road south to Dogashima for the sea caves and dramatic volcanic rock formations. An overnight ryokan stay with a private open-air bath overlooking the ocean is the definitive Izu experience.

Snow Monkey Country
Mountains & Wildlife · Nagano Prefecture

Nagano & Snow Monkeys

長野 · 地獄谷野猿公苑

Nagano Prefecture is one of Japan's most dramatic mountain landscapes — a landlocked region of soaring peaks, deep river valleys, and ancient temple towns that hosted the 1998 Winter Olympics and remains one of the country's premier destinations for winter travel. The star attraction is Jigokudani Monkey Park, set in a steep snow-covered valley north of Nagano city, where a troop of wild Japanese macaques descends from the forest each winter morning to soak in a natural hot spring pool — one of the most extraordinary wildlife spectacles in Japan and unlike anything else in East Asia. The nearby town of Yudanaka is a classic hot spring resort, and Zenko-ji Temple in Nagano city, one of Japan's most important Buddhist pilgrimage sites, is a magnificent stop in its own right.

💡 The snow monkeys are present year-round but the iconic images — macaques submerged to their shoulders in steaming water surrounded by deep snow — happen from December through March. Terry recommends taking the early Shinkansen, reaching Jigokudani by 9am before the day-trippers arrive, then spending the afternoon at Zenko-ji and finishing with a soak at a Yudanaka ryokan. An overnight stay in a traditional inn here is one of the most memorable experiences in Japan.

Japan's Cultural Heart
Imperial City · Kyoto Prefecture

Kyoto

京都 · Capital City

Kyoto was Japan's imperial capital for over a thousand years and remains the country's most concentrated repository of traditional culture — a city of 1,600 Buddhist temples, 400 Shinto shrines, 17 UNESCO World Heritage Sites, geisha districts, imperial palaces, zen rock gardens, and some of the finest kaiseki cuisine in the world. With two and a quarter hours by Shinkansen from Tokyo, it is the most rewarding single long day trip or overnight destination from the capital. The city is large but its historic areas — Higashiyama, Arashiyama, Fushimi, Nishiki — are distinct enough that a well-planned itinerary with Terry can take in three or four of them in a single focused day.

💡 Terry's Kyoto day covers the pre-dawn approach to Fushimi Inari (before the crowds), Nishiki Market for breakfast, the Higashiyama temple walk, and a late afternoon in the Philosopher's Path — finishing with dinner in Gion as the lanterns come on. Staying overnight unlocks early morning Arashiyama bamboo, one of the most extraordinary experiences in Japan.

06
Japan's Kitchen
Food City · Osaka Prefecture

Osaka

大阪 · Great Hill

Osaka is Japan's most extroverted city — louder, friendlier, and hungrier than Tokyo, with a food culture so celebrated that the phrase kuidaore ("eat until you drop") is the city's unofficial motto. The historic castle, the neon-lit Dotonbori canal district, the covered shopping streets of Shinsaibashi, and the underground food universe of Kuromon Market together make Osaka one of the most immediately enjoyable cities in Japan. It pairs naturally with Kyoto, just 15 minutes away by Shinkansen, making a combined overnight trip that covers Japan's two great contrasting urban personalities — refined imperial culture and joyful street-level energy — in a single journey.

💡 Terry's Osaka essentials: takoyaki octopus balls from Wanaka, a sunset view from Osaka Castle park, the Dotonbori walk at dusk, and a proper kushikatsu dinner in Shinsekai. Don't leave without trying okonomiyaki — Osaka's savoury layered pancake — at a restaurant where they cook it at your table.

Japan's First Capital
Ancient Capital · Nara Prefecture

Nara

奈良 · Place of Peace

Nara was Japan's first permanent capital (710–784 AD) and contains a remarkable concentration of ancient Buddhist temples, Shinto shrines, and artifacts from Japan's earliest recorded history — including Todaiji, the world's largest wooden structure, which houses a 15-metre bronze Buddha cast in the 8th century. The central Nara Park, a vast green space connecting the major historic sites, is home to over 1,000 freely roaming sika deer that have been considered sacred messengers of the gods since the city's founding and show a disconcerting willingness to bow in exchange for the deer crackers sold throughout the park. Nara combines best with Kyoto or Osaka as part of an overnight trip to the Kansai region.

💡 The deer are charming but assertive — hold your cracker bag close and buy only what you can feed quickly. Terry recommends the quieter eastern path from Todaiji through Kasuga Grand Shrine and into the ancient forested hillside beyond, where the deer thin out and the 1,300-year-old lantern-lined paths feel genuinely unchanged. Late afternoon light through the shrine's cedar forest is extraordinary.
