Nikkō

Gold shrines, sacred cedar forests & mountain lakes.

Explore stops ↓ Book a tour
Futarasan · Kanmangafuchi · Kegon Falls · Rinnoji · Shinkyo Bridge · Tosho-gu

Nikko is where the Edo shogunate built its most extravagant monument — the Tosho-gu shrine complex, a UNESCO World Heritage site of almost hallucinatory gold and lacquer excess buried in a cedar forest two hours north of Tokyo. But Nikko is more than its most famous shrine: a sacred mountain town with over 1,200 years of religious history, it also holds a turquoise gorge, one of Japan's finest waterfalls, a riverside walk lined with stone Jizo statues, and a mountain lake ringed by volcanic peaks that turns a brilliant red in November.

Oldest Shrine
Shinto Shrine · Founded 782 AD

Futarasan Shrine

二荒山神社

Founded in 782 AD by Shodo Shonin and dedicated to the three peaks of the Nikko mountains, Futarasan is the oldest of Nikko's three main shrines and the one that feels most genuinely sacred — less ornate than Tosho-gu, more deeply embedded in the cedar forest, its stone lanterns moss-covered and its pathways worn smooth by 1,200 years of pilgrims' feet. The shrine's inner garden contains the "Bake-doro" — the Goblin Lantern — a large bronze lantern said to be haunted, with sword marks on its surface from a samurai who attacked it in the night believing it to be a demon. The garden also holds a well of sacred spring water believed to bring good fortune in love.

💡 Futarasan is included in the combined Nikko shrine ticket and is often rushed through by visitors eager to reach Tosho-gu — which means it's the most peaceful stop in the complex. The inner garden (¥200 additional) is worth the extra charge for the sacred spring and the Goblin Lantern. Allow 45 minutes.

The Haunted Jizo Walk
Gorge Walk · Kanmangafuchi

Kanmangafuchi Abyss

含満ヶ淵

A twenty-minute walk from the shrine complex brings you to Kanmangafuchi — a deep gorge carved by ancient volcanic eruptions through which the turquoise Daiya River flows between black basalt walls. The path along the gorge is lined with approximately 70 stone Jizo statues — the Buddhist guardian of travellers and children — placed here by monks over several centuries and now moss-covered and slightly tilted at different angles, giving the path a gently eerie quality that the crowds in the shrine complex entirely dispel. Local legend says that when you count the statues going in one direction and then count again coming back, you always get a different number.

💡 Kanmangafuchi is one of Nikko's most beautiful and least crowded spots — the 20-minute walk from the shrine area keeps most tour groups away. Go in the late afternoon when the light comes low through the cedar and the moss on the Jizo statues glows green. The gorge walk takes about 30 minutes at a gentle pace; the river below the path is a beautiful turquoise year-round.

Japan's Finest Waterfall
Waterfall & Lake · Lake Chuzenji

Kegon Falls & Lake Chuzenji

華厳滝 · 中禅寺湖

A bus ride up the spectacular switchback Irohazaka road — 48 hairpin bends ascending 400 metres in 9 kilometres — brings you to Lake Chuzenji, a caldera lake at 1,269 metres surrounded by volcanic peaks, and to Kegon Falls, which drops 97 metres from the lake's edge over a basalt cliff in one of the three waterfalls traditionally considered the finest in Japan. An elevator descends to an observation platform at the base of the falls, where the spray and the sound are overwhelming. In November the surrounding maple and beech trees turn every shade of red, orange, and gold, making this the finest autumn colour viewing in the Nikko area and one of the most celebrated seasonal landscapes in Japan.

💡 The Irohazaka road is one-way going up and one-way going down — buses run frequently from Nikko Station. Time the visit to arrive at the falls by 3 PM to have time to walk the lakeshore before the last bus back. The observation elevator (¥570) is worth it — the view from the top is dramatic but the base view is extraordinary. In autumn book buses in advance as demand is intense.

Rinnoji Temple

輪王寺

Founded by Shodo Shonin in 766 AD — nearly 900 years before Tosho-gu was built — Rinnoji is the oldest institution on the mountain and the head temple of the Tendai Buddhist sect in the Nikko area. The main hall, Sanbutsudo, houses three enormous gilded Buddhist statues standing 8 metres tall, their gold surfaces gleaming in the dim hall in a display of religious art that rivals anything in Kyoto. The Taiyuin-byo mausoleum complex within the Rinnoji precincts — the tomb of the third Tokugawa shogun Iemitsu — is architecturally comparable to Tosho-gu but sees far fewer visitors, giving it an atmosphere of genuine reverence.

💡 The Treasure Hall (Homotsu-den) beside the main hall contains an extraordinary collection of Buddhist art, ritual objects, and treasures accumulated over 1,200 years. The Shoyoen garden behind the temple is a beautifully maintained stroll garden with a pond and tea house — worth 30 minutes and often nearly empty.

Shinkyo — The Sacred Bridge

神橋

The approach to Nikko's sacred precinct begins at the Shinkyo — a vermillion lacquered bridge arching over the Daiya River that has marked the boundary between the secular and sacred worlds here since the 8th century. According to legend, the holy priest Shodo Shonin crossed the river here on the backs of two giant serpents sent by the god Jinja Daioh when he founded the first temple on the mountain in 766 AD. The current bridge dates from 1636. It sits at the point where the old Nikko Kaido highway from Edo ended and the mountain path to the shrines began — a threshold that still carries a distinct charge even in the age of tour buses and selfie sticks.

💡 The bridge itself charges a small admission to cross (¥300) but the view from the riverbank is free and arguably better. In autumn the maple trees on both banks turn brilliant red and orange, framing the red lacquer of the bridge in colours that seem almost artificially vivid. The 20-minute walk up the cedar avenue from the bridge to the shrine complex sets the atmosphere perfectly.

The Gold Shrine
Shinto Shrine · UNESCO World Heritage · Built 1636

Tosho-gu Shrine

東照宮

Tosho-gu is the mausoleum of Tokugawa Ieyasu — the shogun who unified Japan in 1603 — and the most ornately decorated shrine complex in Japan, possibly in the world. Built in 1636 by his grandson Iemitsu using the labour of 15,000 craftsmen over two years, the complex covers 22 structures decorated with over 5,000 carved and gilded sculptures, 2.4 million sheets of gold leaf, and lacquerwork of extraordinary complexity. The Yomeimon gate — the "Gate of Sundown," so named because visitors could spend all day admiring it — is covered in 508 individual carvings of flowers, animals, and mythological creatures. The famous three wise monkeys (see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil) are carved above the sacred stable here.

💡 Allow at least two hours for the full complex — the route leads through multiple gate courtyards and up 207 stone steps to the relatively simple tomb of Ieyasu at the top, which is the deliberately austere counterpoint to everything below it. The Nemuri-neko (Sleeping Cat) carving above one of the inner gates is one of the most famous single carvings in Japan — easy to miss if you don't know to look up.
