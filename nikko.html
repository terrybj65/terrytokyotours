Nikko is where the Edo shogunate built its most extravagant monument — the Tosho-gu shrine complex, a UNESCO World Heritage site of almost hallucinatory gold and lacquer excess buried in a cedar forest two hours north of Tokyo[cite: 8]. But Nikko is more than its most famous shrine: a sacred mountain town with over 1,200 years of religious history, it also holds a turquoise gorge, one of Japan's finest waterfalls, a riverside walk lined with stone Jizo statues, and a mountain lake ringed by volcanic peaks that turns a brilliant red in November[cite: 8].

Shinkyo — The Sacred Bridge

神橋

The approach to Nikko's sacred precinct begins at the Shinkyo — a vermillion lacquered bridge arching over the Daiya River that has marked the boundary between the secular and sacred worlds here since the 8th century[cite: 8]. According to legend, the holy priest Shodo Shonin crossed the river here on the backs of two giant serpents sent by the god Jinja Daioh when he founded the first temple on the mountain in 766 AD[cite: 8]. The current bridge dates from 1636[cite: 8].

💡 The bridge charges a small admission to cross (¥300) but the view from the riverbank is free and arguably better[cite: 8]. In autumn the maple trees frame the red lacquer of the bridge beautifully[cite: 8]. The 20-minute walk up the cedar avenue sets the atmosphere perfectly[cite: 8].

Rinnoji Temple

輪王寺

Founded by Shodo Shonin in 766 AD — nearly 900 years before Tosho-gu was built — Rinnoji is the oldest institution on the mountain and the head temple of the Tendai Buddhist sect in the Nikko area[cite: 8]. The main hall, Sanbutsudo, houses three enormous gilded Buddhist statues standing 8 metres tall, their gold surfaces gleaming in the dim hall in a display of religious art that rivals anything in Kyoto[cite: 8].

💡 The Treasure Hall (Homotsu-den) contains an extraordinary collection accumulated over 1,200 years[cite: 8]. The Shoyoen garden behind the temple is a beautifully maintained stroll garden with a pond and tea house — worth 30 minutes and often nearly empty[cite: 8].

Tosho-gu Shrine

東照宮

Tosho-gu is the mausoleum of Tokugawa Ieyasu — the shogun who unified Japan in 1603 — and the most ornately decorated shrine complex in Japan, possibly in the world[cite: 8]. Built in 1636 by his grandson Iemitsu using the labour of 15,000 craftsmen, the complex covers 22 structures decorated with over 5,000 carved sculptures and 2.4 million sheets of gold leaf[cite: 8]. The famous three wise monkeys are carved above the stable here[cite: 8].

💡 Allow at least two hours for the full complex[cite: 8]. The route leads through multiple gate courtyards and up 207 stone steps to the relatively simple tomb of Ieyasu at the top[cite: 8]. The Nemuri-neko (Sleeping Cat) carving above an inner gate is easy to miss if you don't look up[cite: 8].

Futarasan Shrine

二荒山神社

Founded in 782 AD by Shodo Shonin and dedicated to the three peaks of the Nikko mountains, Futarasan is the oldest of Nikko's three main shrines and the one that feels most genuinely sacred — less ornate than Tosho-gu, more deeply embedded in the cedar forest, its pathways worn smooth by 1,200 years of pilgrims' feet[cite: 8]. The inner garden contains the "Bake-doro" (the Goblin Lantern), a large bronze lantern said to be haunted[cite: 8].

💡 Futarasan is included in the combined Nikko shrine ticket and is often rushed through — which means it's the most peaceful stop in the complex[cite: 8]. The inner garden (¥200 additional) is absolutely worth the extra charge[cite: 8]. Allow 45 minutes[cite: 8].

Kanmangafuchi Abyss

含満ヶ淵

A twenty-minute walk from the shrine complex brings you to Kanmangafuchi — a deep gorge carved by ancient volcanic eruptions through which the turquoise Daiya River flows between black basalt walls[cite: 8]. The path along the gorge is lined with approximately 70 stone Jizo statues placed here by monks over several centuries and now heavily moss-covered[cite: 8]. Local legend says that you always get a different number when counting them coming and going[cite: 8].

💡 Kanmangafuchi is one of Nikko's most beautiful and least crowded spots[cite: 8]. Go in the late afternoon when the light comes low through the cedar and the moss on the Jizo statues glows green[cite: 8]. The gorge walk takes about 30 minutes at a gentle pace[cite: 8].

Kegon Falls & Lake Chuzenji

華厳滝 · 中禅寺湖

A bus ride up the spectacular switchback Irohazaka road — 48 hairpin bends ascending 400 metres in 9 kilometres — brings you to Lake Chuzenji, a caldera lake at 1,269 metres, and to Kegon Falls, which drops 97 metres from the lake's edge over a basalt cliff in one of the three finest waterfalls in Japan[cite: 8]. An elevator descends to an observation platform at the base of the falls where the spray is overwhelming[cite: 8].

💡 Time the visit to arrive at the falls by 3 PM to have time to walk the lakeshore before the last bus back[cite: 8]. The observation elevator (¥570) is absolutely worth it — the base view is extraordinary[cite: 8]. In autumn, book buses well in advance as seasonal demand is intense[cite: 8].
