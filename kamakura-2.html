Egara Tenjin · Hase-dera · Hokokuji · Jomyoji · Tenen Trail · Zuisenji

The Kamakura that most visitors see — the Great Buddha, Tsurugaoka Hachimangu, the main shopping street — is only the surface of a city built on fourteen hills and riddled with ancient temple grounds, hidden valleys, and ridge trails that connect them all. The deeper Kamakura rewards the visitor who arrives early, wears good shoes, and is willing to climb. Here the hydrangea gardens descend into moss-covered valleys; bamboo groves filter the light into green columns; and the hiking trails along the ridgelines give views over the ocean and the temple rooftops that the crowds on the main street never see.

Oldest Shrine in Kamakura
Shinto Shrine · Nikaido · Founded 1104

Egara Tenjin Shrine

荏柄天神社

Founded in 1104 and the oldest surviving shrine in Kamakura, Egara Tenjin is dedicated to Sugawara no Michizane — the scholar deity of learning and the arts — and was the spiritual guardian of the Kamakura shogunate for 150 years. The shrine sits in a quiet wooded enclosure with a main hall designated as an Important Cultural Property; the ancient plum tree in the courtyard blooms white in February and is one of the earliest signs of spring in the area. The surrounding cryptomeria forest gives the shrine an atmosphere of age and stillness that the better-known shrines in central Kamakura, battered by visitor numbers, can rarely match.

💡 Egara Tenjin is particularly associated with students praying for exam success — the ema votive plaques hanging around the main hall are almost all academic in their requests, many written in the careful handwriting of middle school students. The shrine's annual plum festival in late February draws local visitors but few tourists. Entry is free.

Start Here
Temple & Garden · Hase · Founded 736 AD

Hase-dera

長谷寺

Hase-dera is one of the most beautiful temple complexes in the Kanto region — a hillside garden of extraordinary care and layered beauty set above the Enoden railway line on the western side of Kamakura. The main hall houses a 9.18-metre gilded wooden statue of the eleven-faced Kannon, carved from a single camphor tree and one of the largest wooden sculptures in Japan. Behind it, the garden descends through hydrangea paths, koi ponds, a cave sanctuary carved into the hillside, and a belfry terrace with sweeping views over Sagami Bay. In June the hydrangeas — thousands of them in every shade of blue and purple — make this arguably the finest single garden in Kamakura at any season.

💡 Arrive when the temple opens at 8 AM to have the garden largely to yourself — by 10 AM the hydrangea paths in June have queuing systems in place. The cave sanctuary (Benten-kutsu) carved into the cliff behind the main hall is easy to miss but extraordinary: a low tunnel lined with small carvings of Benzaiten, the goddess of music and art, lit by candlelight.

The Bamboo Temple
Rinzai Zen Temple · Eastern Kamakura

Hokokuji — The Bamboo Temple

報国寺 · 竹の庭

Hokokuji is a Rinzai Zen temple in the quieter eastern hills of Kamakura, founded in 1334, and it contains one of the most remarkable garden spaces in Japan: a grove of 2,000 moso bamboo whose canopy filters the light into columns of pale green that shift and whisper with every movement of the air. The bamboo garden is compact — about 100 metres at its deepest — but the quality of light inside it is unlike anywhere else in Kamakura. A small matcha tea house operates at the garden's heart, where you can sit on a low bench with a bowl of tea and listen to the sound of bamboo. The experience is as close to a living Zen meditation as a garden can provide.

💡 The bamboo garden charges a small separate admission (¥300) on top of the temple entry. The matcha tea set (¥600) includes a seasonal wagashi sweet and is served in the garden itself — one of the finest settings for a bowl of tea in Japan. Arrive early on weekdays for near-solitude inside the grove; weekend afternoons can be crowded.

End of Day
Rinzai Zen Temple · Eastern Kamakura

Jomyoji

浄妙寺

Jomyoji is a Rinzai Zen temple of the same eastern Kamakura cluster as Hokokuji, smaller and quieter and considerably less visited — which makes it a perfect final stop on a day that has already covered considerable ground. The temple grounds are beautifully maintained, with a moss garden, a series of raked gravel courts, and old stone lanterns under the cedars that catch the late afternoon light in ways that reward sitting still for a while. The Ishikawa-tei café, housed in a traditional building within the temple grounds, serves matcha and seasonal sweets in a setting of complete calm — an ideal place to rest before the bus back to Kamakura Station and the train to Tokyo.

💡 Jomyoji is rarely crowded even on weekends — it lies just far enough from the main tourist circuit to deter most visitors. The Ishikawa-tei café (closed Thursdays) serves its matcha set overlooking the moss garden and is one of the most peaceful places to sit in all of Kamakura. The walk between Hokokuji and Jomyoji takes about ten minutes through a quiet residential lane.

Best Ridge Walk
Hiking Trail · Eastern Kamakura Hills

Tenen Hiking Trail

天園ハイキングコース

The Tenen Trail is the finest hiking route in the Kamakura hills — a 5.8-kilometre ridge path running from Zuisenji in the east to Kenchoji in the north-west, crossing the highest point in the Kamakura hills at 159 metres and passing through cedar and oak forest, over rocky outcrops with views toward the ocean, and past the ruins of medieval fortifications and cave tombs. The full trail takes two to three hours at a comfortable pace; shorter sections can be walked from either end. The trailhead near Hokokuji makes it easy to combine the bamboo temple with the beginning of the ridge walk, continuing west along the hills before descending to Kenchoji or Zuisenji.

💡 The best views on the trail are from the rocky summit area near Taiheki-yama, about halfway along — on clear days you can see both Sagami Bay to the south and the hills of the Miura Peninsula across the water. Wear proper shoes; the trail is well-maintained but has muddy sections after rain. Carry water — there are no facilities on the trail itself.

Hidden Valley Garden
Zen Garden · Eastern Kamakura · Founded 1327

Zuisenji

瑞泉寺

Zuisenji sits at the head of a narrow valley in the eastern hills, reached by a long approach path through bamboo and cedar that prepares the visitor for the extraordinary garden that lies at the end of it. Founded in 1327 by the Zen master Muso Soseki — who also designed the famous gardens at Tenryuji in Kyoto — the garden at Zuisenji is considered one of the finest medieval Zen gardens in Japan, cut directly into the rock face of the valley with cave meditation chambers, a lotus pond, and rock-cut channels that still flow with spring water. In February the plum blossoms here are among the earliest in Kamakura; in autumn the maple trees flame over the moss-covered garden floor.

💡 Zuisenji is one of Kamakura's least-visited major temples and is often nearly empty even in peak season — the approach path through the valley puts off visitors who find it too far from the station. This is entirely to your advantage. The rock-cut cave at the back of the garden, where Muso Soseki meditated, is the oldest surviving structure on the site and freely accessible.
