A culinary & cultural journey

In Tokyo, food is never just food. A fish market is a social institution. A basement food hall is an art form. A sake bar is a philosophy lesson. This tour threads together the tastes, rituals, and stories that make Tokyo one of the world's great food cities — from morning tuna to evening sake, with culture woven through every stop.

The Food & Culture Itinerary
01
Street Market
Open-Air Market · Ueno

Ameyoko Market

アメ横商店街

Running beneath the Yamanote Line tracks between Ueno and Okachimachi stations, Ameyoko is Tokyo in concentrated, unfiltered form. Its name comes from the postwar black market that operated here — "ame" referring both to candy (one of the few goods freely available) and to America (the source of much contraband). Today it's a 500-metre stretch of fresh seafood, dried goods, cheap cosmetics, sportswear and street food, all sold by vendors with voices louder than the trains passing overhead. It's raw, vibrant, and entirely real.

💡 New Year's Eve draws massive crowds to Ameyoko as vendors slash prices on perishables. At any time of year, the basement stalls under the tracks carry the best selection of dried seafood and pantry goods.

02
Food Hall
Department Store Basement · Shibuya

Depachika — Shibuya Tokyu Food Show

デパ地下 · 渋谷東急フードショー

Depachika — department store basement food halls — are one of Japan's great contributions to food culture, and the Shibuya Tokyu Food Show is among the finest. Spread across the basement level of Shibuya's main Tokyu store, it's an immaculately curated world of wagyu bento, hand-rolled sushi, matcha confections, artisan pickles, regional sake, and every category of Japanese sweet you've never heard of. It's part supermarket, part museum, part theatre — watching the counter staff prepare food with the same precision as a Michelin kitchen, for a takeaway box, is genuinely moving.

💡 Visit around 6–7 PM when many stalls reduce prices on prepared food significantly. The small communal seating area lets you assemble a multi-stall tasting feast for a fraction of restaurant prices.

03
Omakase Counter
Intimate Counter Restaurant · Meguro

Mark's Tokyo

マークス東京

If the depachika is Tokyo's relationship with food as craft, Mark's Tokyo is its relationship with food as conversation. Chef Mark runs a tiny omakase counter in Meguro where guests sit directly at the kitchen pass and watch every course prepared in front of them. He sources from Japanese farms with a seasonality so strict the menu changes weekly, then explains each ingredient's origin with the enthusiasm of someone who genuinely can't stop thinking about food. By the end of dinner, you'll have made friends with the chef and probably the strangers beside you.

💡 Book well in advance — Mark's is one of Tokyo's worst-kept secrets among food-serious visitors. Seats at the counter book out weeks ahead. Mention any dietary needs when you reserve; he'll adapt without fuss.

04
Cultural Pause
Shinto Shrine · Bunkyo

Nezu Shrine

根津神社

Every great food tour needs a cultural counterpoint — a moment to slow down and understand what you're actually tasting. Nezu Shrine, one of Tokyo's oldest, provides it. Founded in 1706, it sits in a quiet residential neighbourhood and receives a fraction of Meiji Shrine's crowds. A tunnel of vermilion torii gates winds up a wooded hillside — on a smaller, more intimate scale than Kyoto's Fushimi Inari, but more peaceful for it. A moss-covered garden with a koi pond and small waterfall lies beyond. The contrast with the markets you've just left is the whole point.

💡 Draw an omikuji fortune slip for ¥100 at the stand near the main hall — it's a genuine Shinto ritual, not a tourist gimmick. In April, the shrine's azalea festival transforms the hillside into a wall of pink and purple.

05
Evening Sake
Sake Bar · Kōenji

Sake Bar KoKoN

酒場ここん

The perfect ending. Tucked into a Kōenji basement, KoKoN is run by an owner whose knowledge of sake is encyclopaedic and whose enthusiasm for sharing it is boundless. The menu changes daily — he sources bottles that genuinely surprise even experienced sake drinkers, from cloudy nigori that smells of fresh cheese to clear junmai that tastes of mountain water. Flights are curated around your palate in real time. The industrial-warm interior, the communal table, the thin elegant stemware — every detail has been considered. By the end of the night, you'll understand sake differently.

💡 Go without a strong preconception of what sake should taste like — the owner's whole point is to challenge it. The small food dishes, especially the seasonal snacks, are worth ordering alongside the flights.

06
Morning Market
Fish Market · Chuo

Tsukiji Outer Market

築地場外市場

The inner wholesale market moved to Toyosu in 2018, but the outer market — the part that actually matters to visitors — never went anywhere. A dense grid of narrow lanes packed with seafood stalls, tamagoyaki vendors, knife shops and tiny counter restaurants, Tsukiji has been feeding Tokyo since 1935. Grilled scallops straight from the shell, toro sushi at a six-seat counter, thick-cut tamagoyaki on a stick — breakfast here is unlike any other in the world. The traders who work these lanes have done so for generations, and it shows.

💡 Arrive before 10 AM for the best selection and energy. Walk the whole market before buying — prices vary wildly between stalls. Shops close by 1–2 PM, so mornings are essential.
