Kawagoe · Kichijoji · Kokubunji · Machida · Nakano · Tachikawa

Tokyo doesn't end at the Yamanote Line. Spread across the Musashino Plateau and the Tama River basin, the city's true suburbs are where most Tokyoites actually live — and where the city reveals a quieter, more layered version of itself. Castle towns, century-old covered shotengai, riverside parks, jazz coffee shops, and one of the largest public art installations in Japan: this is the Tokyo that exists when nobody is performing for the camera.

Antique Hunting
Antiques & Vintage · South Exit

The Antique District

骨董品街 · 国分寺

The streets south of Kokubunji Station have accumulated one of the densest concentrations of antique and second-hand shops in the western suburbs, drawing dealers and collectors from across the city. The shops range from formal antique dealers handling Meiji-era ceramics and woodblock prints to casual junk shops piled floor-to-ceiling with old kitchenware, Showa-era furniture, and miscellaneous curiosities. Prices and finds are considerably better here than anything you'll encounter in Shimokitazawa or central Tokyo.

💡 The monthly flea market at Kokubunji Hikawa Shrine draws vendors from across the region — check dates before visiting. Combine with a stop at one of the surviving jazz kissaten nearby for the full Kokubunji experience.

Street Art
Public Art · Faret Tachikawa

Faret Tachikawa

ファーレ立川

Faret Tachikawa is a public art project embedded into the redeveloped area north of the station — 109 works by 92 artists from 36 countries, installed across plazas, pavements, car parks, and building facades of an otherwise unremarkable suburban shopping district. The result is genuinely strange and wonderful: round a corner and find a full-scale military jet embedded in a building facade, or a fire hydrant reimagined as sculpture, or a maintenance hatch turned into art. Most people walk past the majority of works without noticing — which is partly the point.

💡 Pick up the official art map from the information desk near the station's north exit — it's the only way to find all 109 works. The scavenger-hunt quality of finding them all makes for one of the more enjoyable afternoons in the western suburbs.
★★★★ 4.1 · 2,900+ reviews

03
Jazz Alley
Food, Drink & Music · Harmonica Yokocho

Harmonica Yokocho

ハーモニカ横丁

Just north of Kichijoji Station, a postwar black-market alley has survived into the 21st century as one of Tokyo's most charming drinking and dining lanes. Named for its grid of tiny alley slots resembling harmonica holes, it's a labyrinth of 70-odd tiny bars, yakitori counters, jazz listening rooms, and standing izakayas — many running in the same spot for 50 years. The lanes are so narrow that you could reach out and touch both walls. It opens early and closes very late.

💡 Lunch in the alley is quieter and cheaper than evenings — several shops open from noon. The jazz kissaten (listening cafés) in and around Harmonica Yokocho are among the last surviving examples of a uniquely Japanese institution: find one, sit down, order a coffee.

Nakano

中野 · Nakano Ward Otaku & Retro 🚃 ~8 min from Shinjuku · Chuo Line
Nakano is Akihabara's quieter, more approachable cousin — a suburb where manga, anime, and retro toy culture runs just as deep but without the crowds or the neon. The Broadway shopping complex is legendary among collectors worldwide, and the shotengai outside it is one of the best-preserved covered arcades in Tokyo.
04
Park Life
Park & Pond · Inokashira

Inokashira Park

井の頭恩賜公園

A 37-hectare public park centred on a large natural pond, Inokashira has been Kichijoji's living room for over a century. Rowing boats can be hired by the hour, the path around the pond takes about twenty minutes at a gentle pace, and the Benzaiten shrine sits on a small island in the middle of the water. On weekends the paths fill with musicians, artists selling work, and families who have been coming here every Sunday for thirty years. In late March it's one of the finest cherry blossom spots in the entire metro area.

💡 The Ghibli Museum is a ten-minute walk through the park's west end — book tickets months in advance as they sell out immediately. Even without a ticket, the walk through this corner of the park is lovely.

Street Snacks
Food & Culture · Kashiya Yokocho

Kashiya Yokocho — Candy Lane

菓子屋横丁

A short walk from the kurazukuri district, Kashiya Yokocho is a narrow lane of old wooden sweet shops selling traditional Japanese confectionery since the Meiji era. Around 20 shops remain, all selling handmade dagashi, ramune candy, mochi, and the sweet potato products Kawagoe has been famous for since the Edo period. The wooden shopfronts are original, the recipes unchanged, and the prices low enough to try everything without thinking twice.

💡 Most shops let you sample before buying. The lane is about 200 metres long — walk the full length before committing to anything. The sweet potato soft-serve stall at the entrance is worth the queue.

Kichijoji

吉祥寺 · Musashino City Most Liveable 🚃 ~15 min from Shinjuku · Chuo Line
Kichijoji regularly tops polls of Tokyo's most desirable place to live, and spending a day here makes it easy to understand why. It has everything in the right proportions: a large park, a covered shotengai, excellent independent restaurants and jazz bars, and Inokashira Pond with its rowing boats and shrine — all within ten minutes of the station.
06
Little Edo
Historic District · Kurazukuri

Kurazukuri Street

蔵造りの街並み

The kurazukuri district is a 400-metre stretch of Edo-period clay-walled merchant warehouses that have traded continuously since the 18th century. The thick earthen walls were built to resist the fires that repeatedly destroyed other Kanto towns, and their survival is a stroke of extraordinary historical luck. Ground floors house craft shops, confectionery, and sake dealers while the upper floors remain essentially unchanged. The Toki no Kane bell tower at the north end rings four times daily, just as it has for 400 years.

💡 Arrive by 9:30 AM before tour groups arrive from Tokyo. Start at the bell tower and work south, stopping into the free museum buildings along the way. The sweet potato soft-serve (imo) is Kawagoe's signature — try it.
★★★★★ 4.5 · 18,000+ reviews

07
Shotengai Town
Shopping Street · Central Machida

Machida Shotengai District

町田仲見世商店街

Machida's network of covered and semi-covered shopping streets radiating from the station is among the most intact examples of postwar shotengai culture in the southern suburbs. Several distinct arcades connect into a continuous zone: the Nakamise covered lane, and the quieter backstreets lined with independent ramen shops, used CD stores, and clothing outlets. What makes Machida's shotengai distinct from smarter suburban centres is its lack of pretension — the shops are practical, the customers local, the prices honest. The second-hand book and music district east of the station is particularly good.

💡 Machida has a notably strong used CD and vinyl culture, with several dedicated shops in the backstreets east of the station carrying a wider selection at lower prices than central Tokyo equivalents.

08
Collector's Paradise
Pop Culture · Nakano Broadway

Nakano Broadway

中野ブロードウェイ

A multi-storey shopping complex that has been the spiritual home of Tokyo's otaku collector culture since the 1980s, when Mandarake — Japan's largest used manga and anime goods chain — opened its first store here. The upper floors are an almost entirely self-contained universe of used manga, vintage anime figures, retro game cartridges, rare trading cards, and cult film memorabilia. Unlike Akihabara, the crowds are manageable, prices are often better, and the atmosphere is absorbed rather than touristy.

💡 The ground floor is an ordinary supermarket — take the escalators up to floors 2–4 for the collector shops. Mandarake occupies numerous separate units, each specialising in a different category. Allow at least two hours.

Shotengai Life
Shopping Street · Sun Mall

Nakano Sun Mall

中野サンモール

The 230-metre covered shopping arcade connecting Nakano Station's north exit to Broadway is one of the finest examples of a working postwar shotengai still operating as intended — a neighbourhood high street rather than a tourist attraction. Family-run lunch spots, independent clothing stores, a fishmonger, a traditional wagashi sweet shop, a standing soba counter, and a wide variety of small businesses have been trading here since the 1950s. At lunchtime it fills with local workers; on Saturday mornings it's families doing the weekly shop.

💡 Shotengai lunch sets inside the arcade are some of the best-value meals in the western suburbs — rice, miso, a main, and pickles for ¥800–1,000. Look for the handwritten daily specials boards at each restaurant entrance.

Kokubunji

国分寺 · Kokubunji City Antiques & Jazz 🚃 ~25 min from Shinjuku · Chuo Line
Kokubunji has a reputation among Tokyo locals as a quiet, slightly eccentric suburb with an unusually good concentration of antique shops, jazz kissaten, and old-fashioned coffee houses. It lacks the polish of Kichijoji but more than makes up for it in authentic neighbourhood character — and some of the best second-hand furniture and ceramics hunting in the region.
10
Ancient Ruins
Park & History · Musashi Kokubunji

Nogawa Park & Temple Ruins

野川公園 · 武蔵国分寺跡

A fifteen-minute walk south of the station brings you to the ruins of Musashi Kokubunji — an 8th-century provincial temple complex established by Emperor Shomu, whose earthwork footings and stone lanterns remain across a wide grassy site. Adjacent Nogawa Park follows the Nogawa River through woodland and open meadow, with walking and cycling paths that feel entirely removed from the city. The juxtaposition of 1,300-year-old temple ruins with a suburban river walk is distinctly, pleasantly Kokubunji.

💡 The Musashi Kokubunji ruins museum (¥100 entry) gives good context before walking the site. In early spring the surrounding area has excellent plum blossoms; the maple trees along Nogawa in autumn are quietly spectacular.

Machida

町田 · Machida City South Suburb 🚃 ~35 min from Shinjuku · Odakyu Line
Machida sits at the southern edge of Tokyo Prefecture, closer in spirit to Kanagawa than to central Tokyo. It's a proper mid-size suburban city with a long shotengai culture, a strong used-book and music scene, a beautiful designated national garden, and a café culture that has grown up around its two universities.
11
Best Park in Tokyo
National Park · Showa Kinen

Showa Kinen Park

国営昭和記念公園

Built on the site of a former US Air Force base and opened in 1983, Showa Kinen Park covers 165 hectares of landscaped gardens, cycling paths, forest walks, and seasonal flower fields. Each season brings a different spectacle: tulips and rapeseed in spring, sunflowers in summer, cosmos fields and ginkgo avenues in autumn, and illuminations in winter. The cycling rental service lets you cover the park's full extent, and the Japanese garden at the centre — with its formal pond, tea ceremony house, and raked gravel garden — is as good as anything in Kyoto.

💡 The cosmos fields in October are extraordinary — a sea of pink and white stretching to the horizon. The autumn ginkgo avenue in mid-November is equally worth a special trip. Rent a bicycle at the West Gate and allow at least three hours.

12
Garden Retreat
Garden & Nature · Yakushi Pond Park

Yakushi Pond Park

薬師池公園

A short bus ride from Machida Station, Yakushi Pond Park is one of Tokyo's designated "100 Famous Gardens" and one of the least-visited. A large natural pond sits at the centre of a traditional garden with teahouses, a working watermill, iris beds, and a grove of old plum trees that bloom in late January and February. The surrounding park extends into woodland walks. It's the kind of place you don't expect to find in the outer suburbs — a genuinely serene garden that rewards the slight effort to reach it.

💡 Plum blossom season (mid-January to mid-February) and iris season (mid-June) are the most spectacular times to visit. The park teahouse serves matcha and seasonal wagashi — combine with a slow walk around the pond before heading back.
★★★★★ 4.5 · 4,200+ reviews

Tachikawa

立川 · Tachikawa City Parks & Art 🚃 ~35 min from Shinjuku · Chuo Line
Tachikawa is the commercial hub of the Tama region. It repays a stop of its own: Showa Kinen Park is one of the largest and most spectacular public parks in the entire metropolitan area, and Faret Tachikawa is one of the strangest and most interesting public art installations in Japan — 109 works embedded invisibly into an ordinary suburban shopping district.
