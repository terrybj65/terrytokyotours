Asakusa
Old Tokyo

Temples, incense, rickshaws & the Sumida River.

Hoppy Street · Kaminarimon · Nakamise · Senso-ji · Sky Tree · Sumida

Asakusa is the part of Tokyo that was already old when the rest of the city was still rice paddies. Built around Senso-ji — Tokyo's oldest and most visited temple, founded in 628 AD — the district has been the city's spiritual and entertainment centre for over a millennium. Rickshaws, paper lanterns, hand-pulled sembei, traditional craft workshops, incense smoke drifting over ancient courtyards: Asakusa holds the texture of old Edo more completely than anywhere else in the modern city, and does so without feeling like a museum. It's still a living neighbourhood, and that vitality is what makes it extraordinary.

Hoppy Street

ホッピー通り

Running west from the Nitenmon gate of Senso-ji, Hoppy Street is a short lane of open-fronted izakayas and food stalls that has been feeding and watering Asakusa visitors since the postwar period. The street is named after Hoppy — a low-alcohol beer substitute invented in 1948 when real beer was too expensive for ordinary Tokyoites — which is still served here mixed with shochu in the traditional way. Plastic chairs and tables spill onto the street; the food is classic izakaya — motsuni (offal stew), yakitori, oden — and the atmosphere in the early evening, with the last light falling over the temple neighbourhood and the first lanterns coming on, is as good as anywhere in the city.

💡 Arrive at 5 PM when the restaurants open and the street is at its most relaxed. Order Hoppy with shochu (ask for "naka" for the shochu refill and "soto" for the Hoppy itself — the glass and ice stay, only the liquid changes). The motsuni — braised offal stew — is the dish Hoppy Street is known for and costs around ¥500.

Kaminarimon

雷門 · Thunder Gate

Kaminarimon — the Thunder Gate — is the outer gate of Senso-ji and the most recognisable single image in Asakusa: a vast red gate hung with an enormous red paper lantern, flanked by the gods of wind and thunder in their niches. The current gate dates from 1960, funded by the founder of Panasonic, Konosuke Matsushita, in gratitude for his recovery from illness after praying at Senso-ji. The lantern, which weighs 670 kg and is 3.9 metres tall, is replaced every decade. Beyond the gate, the Nakamise shopping street runs north to the temple's inner gate — one of the oldest continuously operating shopping streets in Japan.

💡 The underside of the giant lantern is decorated with a large carved dragon — easy to miss in the crowds but worth looking up for. The best photographs of the gate are taken from the south, looking north with the temple pagoda visible over the roofline beyond.

Oldest Shopping Street
Shopping Street · Between the Gates

Nakamise-dori

仲見世通り

The 250-metre shopping street connecting Kaminarimon to Senso-ji's inner Hozomon gate has been selling traditional snacks, souvenirs, and craft goods to temple visitors for over 1,000 years — making it arguably the oldest continuously operating shopping street in Japan. The 89 stalls lining both sides of the covered lane sell ningyo-yaki (small bean paste cakes moulded into temple shapes), kaminari-okoshi (puffed rice brittle that has been made in Asakusa since the Edo period), traditional paper fans, wooden geta sandals, and a remarkable quantity of beautifully packaged regional sweets. The stalls are small, the shopkeepers are often elderly, and the merchandise is overwhelmingly edible or handmade.

💡 The side streets running east and west off Nakamise — particularly Shin-Nakamise — hold smaller, quieter shops with better prices and fewer crowds. The traditional craft shops on the western side streets sell genuine Asakusa-made goods: combs, fans, lanterns, and lacquerware at prices that reflect real craft rather than tourist markup.

Senso-ji Temple

浅草寺

Tokyo's oldest temple and its most visited, Senso-ji was founded in 628 AD when two fishermen pulled a golden image of Kannon — the goddess of mercy — from the Sumida River. The current main hall dates from 1958, rebuilt after wartime destruction, but the temple's spiritual authority and its grip on the neighbourhood remain entirely undiminished. The vast main courtyard, the five-storey pagoda rising over the surrounding rooftops, the enormous bronze lanterns, the incense cauldron where worshippers wave smoke over themselves for good health — all of it operates with the full weight of 1,400 years of continuous religious practice behind it.

💡 Arrive before 7 AM to experience the temple grounds in near-silence — the contrast with the midday crowds is extraordinary. Draw an omikuji (fortune slip) from the metal cylinders near the main hall: shake until a numbered stick falls out, then find the corresponding drawer. If you draw a bad fortune, tie it to the rack and leave it at the temple.

Sumida River & Park

隅田川 · 隅田公園

The Sumida River has been Asakusa's eastern boundary and its lifeline since the Edo period, when it carried the rice, timber, and merchandise that fed the growing city. Today the western bank is lined with Sumida Park — a long riverside promenade of cherry trees, benches, and open lawn that gives the best ground-level view of the Tokyo Sky Tree rising across the water. Water buses run from the Asakusa pier south to Hinode and Odaiba, and the 40-minute river journey downstream past the bridges of central Tokyo is one of the most quietly enjoyable ways to see the city from a different angle. In late July, the Sumida Fireworks Festival is one of the oldest and largest in Japan.

💡 The Sumida Park cherry blossoms in late March are exceptional — over 600 trees line the riverbank on both sides. The Tokyo Cruise water bus from Asakusa pier to Odaiba (¥1,720) passes under 12 bridges and gives a completely different view of the city than any train or taxi. Book the earlier departures to avoid the afternoon crowds.

World's Tallest Tower
Observation Tower · Oshiage

Tokyo Sky Tree

東京スカイツリー

Rising 634 metres from the flatlands east of Asakusa, Tokyo Sky Tree is the tallest tower in the world and the defining feature of the eastern Tokyo skyline. The two observation decks — at 350 metres (Tembo Deck) and 450 metres (Tembo Galleria) — give panoramic views stretching to Mount Fuji on clear days and across the entire Tokyo metropolitan area at all times. At night the tower is illuminated in rotating colour schemes, and the view from the upper deck over the city's sea of lights — with the Sumida River winding through it and the glow of Shinjuku and Shibuya visible 15 km to the west — is genuinely breathtaking. The base complex, Solamachi, contains over 300 shops and restaurants.

💡 Book tickets online in advance — queues for same-day tickets can be two hours or more on weekends. Sunset plus one hour is the optimal time: you see the city in daylight, watch the sun go down, and then see the lights come on across the entire metropolis. The Tembo Deck at 350m is genuinely impressive; the Tembo Galleria at 450m adds a glass-floored spiral walkway that is not for the faint-hearted.
