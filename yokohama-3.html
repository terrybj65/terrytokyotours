Day Trip from Tokyo · Area Guide

Yokohama

Japan's most international city — a port, Chinatown, and the open sea. Only 30 minutes from Tokyo but a world apart.

Book this day trip Check availability
横浜 · Yokohama

Yokohama grew up differently from Tokyo — shaped by its port and the foreign traders who settled here from the 1850s onward. That international character still runs through the city today: in its vast Chinatown, its breezy waterfront, and the Meiji-era Western houses perched quietly above the bay. Terry has been coming here for decades and knows exactly where to take you.

Food & Culture Morning · Stop 01
Neighbourhood

Chinatown

横浜中華街
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

Japan's largest Chinatown is a dense, intoxicating world unto itself — nearly 600 restaurants, traditional medicine shops, dim sum parlours, and ornate ceremonial gates draped in red lanterns. The community here has roots going back over 150 years to the opening of Yokohama port, and that depth of history is felt in every alleyway. Terry knows which restaurants the locals actually eat at, not just the tourist traps lining the main boulevard.

💡 Come hungry — the steamed pork buns (nikuman) sold at street stalls here are legendary and should not be skipped under any circumstances.
Architecture & Views Mid-Morning · Stop 02
Waterfront District

Minato Mirai

みなとみらい
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

The sleek, modern face of Yokohama — a reclaimed waterfront district of sweeping plazas, glittering towers, and the iconic 296-metre Landmark Tower. Minato Mirai, meaning "harbour of the future," was built on the site of the old Mitsubishi shipyards and retains fragments of that industrial past alongside its contemporary ambitions. Terry explains the remarkable urban transformation that turned this working port district into one of Japan's most admired city planning projects.

💡 The Cosmo Clock 21 Ferris wheel is beautifully lit at dusk — if your timing works out, it makes for a spectacular end to the afternoon here.
History & Harbour Late Morning · Stop 03
Promenade

Yamashita Park

山下公園
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

Japan's first Western-style seaside promenade stretches along the edge of the bay where Yokohama opened to the world in 1859. The permanent mooring of the Hikawa Maru ocean liner — a retired 1930s passenger ship that once sailed between Yokohama and Seattle — acts as a centrepiece for the park. Stroll the waterfront as Terry recounts the extraordinary story of how this fishing village became Japan's gateway to the West within a single generation.

💡 The Hikawa Maru can be boarded and explored inside — Terry recommends taking 30 minutes to walk its Art Deco interiors, which are remarkably well preserved.
Design & Architecture Afternoon · Stop 04
Landmark

Osanbashi Pier

大さん橋
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

One of the most striking pieces of contemporary architecture in Japan — a vast undulating pier that extends 458 metres into Yokohama Bay, its rooftop forming an open public deck of gently rolling cedar-clad waves. Designed by London-based architects Farshid Moussavi and Alejandro Zaera-Polo, Osanbashi serves as the city's international cruise terminal while functioning as a beloved public park above the water. Walking its timber surface at golden hour, with the Minato Mirai skyline behind you and cargo ships in the distance, is genuinely unforgettable.

💡 The rooftop is always open to the public and free to access — one of the best viewpoints in the entire city, and almost always beautifully uncrowded.
History & Architecture Mid-Afternoon · Stop 05
Hilltop Neighbourhood

Yamate District

山手
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

Climb the bluff above the city to Yamate — the leafy hilltop neighbourhood where Yokohama's foreign residents built their homes in the Meiji era. Elegant Victorian and Edwardian villas still stand along quiet, tree-lined streets, many now open as heritage museums. The contrast with the city below is remarkable: a tranquil, almost European enclave preserved above the noise and density of modern Yokohama. Terry brings the stories of the merchants, diplomats, and missionaries who shaped this quarter to vivid life.

💡 The Harbour View Park at the edge of Yamate offers arguably the finest panoramic view of Yokohama Bay — arrive late afternoon for the best light over the water.
Shopping & Local Life Late Afternoon · Stop 06
Shopping Street

Motomachi

元町
📍 View on Google Maps ↗

Running parallel to the base of the Yamate bluff, Motomachi is Yokohama's most distinctive shopping street — a graceful, low-rise boulevard of independent boutiques, long-established family businesses, and European-influenced café culture that grew up serving the foreign community above. Unlike Tokyo's fashion districts, Motomachi has resisted chain-store homogenisation and retains genuine neighbourhood character. Look for the old craftsmen's workshops tucked between the boutiques, producing leather goods, hats, and tailored clothing by hand as they have for generations.

💡 End the day at one of the French-style bakeries on the street — Motomachi has been famous for its Western-style bread since the Meiji era, and the quality remains exceptional.

Good to Know

Yokohama is best enjoyed as a full-day trip from Tokyo — there's genuinely too much to see in half a day. Comfortable walking shoes are strongly recommended.

Transit costs (Yokohama is approx. 30–45 min from central Tokyo by Tokyu Toyoko Line or JR) are not included in the guiding rate. Terry will advise on the best route from your hotel.

A City That Rewards Curiosity

Most visitors see only Chinatown and Minato Mirai — and miss the quiet layers that make Yokohama genuinely special. Terry has spent decades exploring this city and knows where the real stories are: the side streets of Noge, the old warehouses of Bashamichi, the extraordinary Foreigners' Cemetery on the Yamate bluff. Tell him what interests you most and he'll build a day that goes well beyond the standard itinerary.
