Ancient capital · Coastal escape

For three centuries, Kamakura was Japan's political capital and home to its most powerful samurai clans. Today it's a small coastal city of temples, shrines, forested hiking paths, and one of the most famous statues in Asia — all just an hour from Shinjuku. This is the day trip that explains medieval Japan.

The Kamakura Itinerary
01
Sea Views
Buddhist Temple · Hase

Hase-dera Temple

長谷寺

A five-minute walk from the Great Buddha brings you to Hase-dera, and the contrast is instructive — where Kōtoku-in is singular and monumental, Hasedera is layered and intimate. The temple climbs a hillside in terraces: a pond garden at the base, a cave carved with Buddhist figures midway, and at the top, an open terrace with a sweeping view across Kamakura's rooftops to Yuigahama beach and Sagami Bay beyond. The main hall houses a nine-metre gilded Kannon statue, carved from a single camphor log in the 8th century. Late afternoon, with the light falling across the bay, is one of the most beautiful moments in the Kamakura day.

💡 Arrive 30–45 minutes before closing for quieter grounds and the best light on the coastal view. The cave walk (Benten-kutsu) is narrow and low — watch your head but don't skip it.

02
Street Food
Shopping & Snacking · Central Kamakura

Komachi-dōri

小町通り

Running parallel to the shrine's approach, Komachi-dōri is Kamakura's main commercial artery — and the perfect place for a mid-morning snack before the temples. Skewered grilled seafood, matcha soft-serve spiralling dangerously high in waffle cones, freshly made rice crackers, shirasu (whitebait) on everything — this street does not believe in subtlety. The shops selling lacquerware, ceramics, and locally made crafts balance the food stalls nicely. It's busy, colourful, and happily chaotic.

💡 Look for shops selling Kamakura's local speciality: shirasu-don (whitebait rice bowl). The fishing boats that supply the restaurants work out of nearby Yuigahama and Zaimokuza beaches — it doesn't get fresher.

03
Icon of Kamakura
Buddhist Temple · Hase

Kōtoku-in — The Great Buddha

高徳院 · 鎌倉大仏

No photograph prepares you for the scale or the serenity of the Kamakura Daibutsu. Cast in bronze in 1252, the seated Amida Buddha stands 11.4 metres tall and weighs 121 tonnes. It originally sat inside a large temple hall; that building was destroyed by typhoons and a tsunami in the late 15th century, leaving the statue in the open air, where it has sat ever since — perfectly still, framed by forested hills, radiating a quietness that silences even the busiest tour groups. You can enter the statue itself through small doors in its back for an additional ¥50.

💡 Visit in the morning before midday crowds arrive. The approach path offers a sequence of framed views as the Buddha gradually reveals itself through the temple gate — walk slowly and let it unfold.

04
Start Here
Shinto Shrine · Central Kamakura

Tsurugaoka Hachimangū

鶴岡八幡宮

Begin where Kamakura's story begins. Tsurugaoka Hachimangū was founded in 1063 and rebuilt by the first shogun Minamoto no Yoritomo in 1180, making it the spiritual heart of the entire Kamakura period. A long approach called the Dan-kazura — a raised central path flanked by cherry trees — leads from the sea straight to the shrine steps. The complex sits at the top of broad stone stairs, its vermilion buildings framed against a wooded hillside. On weekends you may witness a traditional wedding procession or a Shinto ceremony — real, unhurried, and open to respectful onlookers.

💡 Arrive by 9 AM before the tour groups. The pond near the entrance has lotus flowers in summer and hosts ducks year-round — a peaceful spot to collect yourself before climbing to the main hall.

05
End of Day
Coastal Beach · Hase

Yuigahama Beach

由比ヶ浜

End the day the right way. Yuigahama is Kamakura's main beach — a long, grey-sand stretch where surfers read the Pacific swells, beach restaurants serve cold Shonan Beer, and the day empties out of its urgency. It's not a tropical postcard beach; it's a Japanese beach, which means it's blustery and overcast as often as it's sunny, and somehow better for it. Walk south along the shore toward Zaimokuza as the light fades, watch the surfers catch the last waves of the evening, and take the train back to Tokyo feeling like you've been somewhere genuinely far away.

💡 The beach restaurants (海の家) set up seasonal huts in summer with food, beer, and deck chairs. Outside summer, you'll often have long stretches entirely to yourself — its own kind of luxury.

06
Hidden Shrine
Shinto Cave Shrine · Western Hills

Zeniarai Benzaiten

銭洗弁財天宇賀福神社

Few places in Japan are as genuinely surprising on arrival. Zeniarai Benzaiten — founded in 1185 — is entered through a tunnel carved through a cliff face. On the other side, a cave complex is filled with flickering candles, incense smoke, and small waterfalls trickling over moss-covered rock. The shrine is dedicated to the goddess of fortune, and its central ritual involves washing banknotes or coins in spring water from the cave — legend says it multiplies whatever you wash. Whether or not you believe it, the atmosphere is unlike anything else in Kamakura.

💡 Bring cash to wash — the ritual requires coins or small notes placed in a bamboo basket and rinsed in the cave spring. The walk up from the main road is a pleasant 10-minute hill climb through a quiet residential neighbourhood.
