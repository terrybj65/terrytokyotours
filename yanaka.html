Yanaka

Temples, cats, shotengai & the slow life of old Tokyo.

Explore stops ↓ Book a tour
Nezu · Sendagi · Yanaka · The Yanesen Triangle

Yanaka is the part of Tokyo that the 20th century largely forgot to demolish. Escaping the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake and the 1945 firebombing that levelled most of the city, the neighbourhood retains a dense fabric of wooden temples, old craftsmen's workshops, narrow winding lanes, and more than 70 Buddhist temple grounds — all within walking distance of each other. Cats sleep on stone lanterns. The shotengai smells of grilled fish and fresh tofu. Old ladies tend flower pots outside wooden houses that have stood since the Meiji era. It is, in the most unguarded way, the most Tokyo-feeling neighbourhood left in Tokyo.

Coffee & Craft · Throughout Yanaka

The Machiya Cafés

町家カフェ · 谷中

Over the last decade, a quiet wave of independent coffee shops and craft studios has moved into Yanaka's old machiya townhouses and converted workshops — not displacing the existing character of the neighbourhood but layering something new on top of something very old. Kayaba Coffee, open since 1938 and still serving its original menu of coffee and egg sandwiches from its original wooden building, is the anchor. Around it, a cluster of smaller roasters, handcraft shops, and pottery studios occupy spaces that feel entirely unhurried. End the day here — with a coffee, a view of a wooden lane, and the sound of the neighbourhood settling into its evening — and Yanaka will have done what it does best: made time feel slower than it actually is.

💡 Kayaba Coffee (open since 1938) is on the corner of a Yanaka backstreet about five minutes' walk from the cemetery — look for the old dark-wood building with the lantern outside. It opens at 8 AM and is best visited at opening or mid-afternoon when the lunchtime queue has cleared. The egg salad sandwich on thick white bread is the thing to order.

Shinto Shrine · Nezu

Nezu Shrine

根津神社

One of Tokyo's oldest and most complete shrines, Nezu Jinja was founded over 1,900 years ago and its current buildings date from 1706 — making them among the oldest surviving structures in the city. The shrine is famous for its tunnel of vermillion torii gates winding up through the wooded hillside behind the main hall, a miniature version of Kyoto's Fushimi Inari that most visitors to Tokyo never discover. In late April and early May the azalea garden on the eastern slope explodes into colour — 3,000 azalea bushes covering the hillside in pink, red, and white — during one of the city's most beautiful and least-publicised seasonal events.

💡 The Nezu Shrine azalea festival (Tsutsuji Matsuri) runs from mid-April to early May and is genuinely spectacular — arrive on a weekday to avoid weekend crowds. The shrine complex is free to enter; the azalea garden charges a small admission during the festival period. The wooden main hall and its surrounding buildings are National Important Cultural Properties.

Art in an Edo Bathhouse
Contemporary Art · SCAI The Bathhouse

SCAI The Bathhouse

SCAI THE BATHHOUSE

Tucked into a Yanaka backstreet, SCAI The Bathhouse is one of Tokyo's most respected contemporary art galleries — housed in a 200-year-old public bathhouse (sento) whose high-ceilinged interior, original tile work, and wooden changing room fittings have been preserved and repurposed into an exhibition space of extraordinary character. The gallery represents some of Japan's most significant contemporary artists — including Kohei Nawa, Tatsuo Miyajima, and Lee Ufan — alongside international names, and the quality and ambition of its programming consistently punches above its small physical size. Entry is free. The contrast between the Edo-period building and the contemporary art inside is perfectly Yanaka.

💡 SCAI is closed Sundays and Mondays and between exhibitions — check the website before visiting. The gallery is genuinely small (one main room plus the former changing area) so allow 30–45 minutes. The neighbourhood around it, particularly the lane immediately west, has several other small galleries worth finding.

Walking · Yanaka & Nezu Backstreets

The Temple Town Backstreets

寺町の路地 · 谷中

The real treasure of Yanaka is not any single destination but the network of narrow lanes threading between the cemetery, the shrine, and the shotengai. More than 70 Buddhist temples are distributed across this small area — most with their gates open, their incense burning, and their grounds freely accessible. Wooden houses with tiled roofs and small gardens line lanes too narrow for cars. Craftsmen's workshops sit beside tiny galleries and coffee shops occupying converted machiya townhouses. Cats sleep on walls. There are no signs telling you where to go, which is the point — the pleasure of Yanaka is in wandering without a map and finding things that weren't in any guidebook.

💡 Pick up a free neighbourhood walking map from the Yanaka information point near Nippori Station. The area between the cemetery and Nezu Shrine — roughly bounded by Yanaka Ginza to the north and Shinobazu-dori to the south — is the densest and most rewarding for aimless exploration. Allow at least two hours.

Cemetery & Cherry Blossoms · Nippori

Yanaka Cemetery

谷中霊園

Yanaka Cemetery is one of Tokyo's oldest public burial grounds, established in 1874 on the site of a former temple complex, and it is one of the most unexpectedly beautiful places in the city. The main avenue — a broad gravel path lined on both sides with enormous cherry trees — is one of the finest hanami spots in Tokyo in late March, when the blossoms create a complete canopy over the graves and the combination of beauty and mortality feels entirely Japanese. The grounds are vast enough to get genuinely lost in, and the mix of old stone lanterns, weathered family monuments, moss-covered graves, and the occasional cat makes it feel like a living document of the city's past 150 years.

💡 The last shogun of the Edo period, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, is buried here — look for the signposted path to his grave in the eastern section. The cemetery is freely accessible at all hours. Morning is the best time: quiet, misty in autumn, and busy only with crows and the occasional elderly visitor tending a family grave.

Best Shotengai in Tokyo
Shopping Street · Yanaka Ginza

Yanaka Ginza

谷中銀座

A 170-metre covered shopping street that has been feeding and supplying the neighbourhood since the postwar period, Yanaka Ginza is often called the most nostalgic shotengai in Tokyo — and it earns the title without effort. Fishmongers, tofu makers, sembei (rice cracker) bakeries, pickle sellers, handmade craft workshops, and a handful of old-fashioned sweet shops line both sides under hand-painted signs that look unchanged since the 1960s. Cats wander between the stalls; locals exchange gossip at doorways; the smell of grilled skewers drifts from the far end. At lunchtime it's at its most alive, with locals doing the shopping they've done here every day for decades.

💡 The stone steps at the far (east) end of Yanaka Ginza — the "Yuyake Dandan," or sunset steps — lead up to a view back over the shotengai rooftops that is one of Yanaka's most photographed moments, particularly at dusk. The steps get their name from the orange light that fills them on clear evenings.
