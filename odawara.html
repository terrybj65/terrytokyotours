📍 Odawara · 45 min from Tokyo

Odawara

Samurai castle, fish markets & the old Tōkaidō.

Explore stops ↓ Book a tour
Odawara Castle · Fish Market · Tokaido · Ninomaru · Kamaboko · Coastal Walk

Odawara is a castle town that most people pass through on the way to Hakone and never stop to explore — which is their loss. The city was the stronghold of the Hojo clan for over a century, and its castle, fish market, old Tokaido highway remnants, and shogunate-era merchant quarters give it a depth of history that few comparable towns in the Kanto region can match. Add one of the finest kamaboko (fish cake) traditions in Japan, a coastal promenade with views to Izu, and some excellent seafood restaurants, and Odawara earns a full day of its own.

Castle · Hojo Clan Stronghold · Rebuilt 1960

Odawara Castle

小田原城

Odawara Castle was the impregnable stronghold of the Hojo clan — the most powerful warlords of the Kanto region — for nearly a century, and the last great castle to fall to Toyotomi Hideyoshi's unification campaign in 1590. The current keep is a 1960 concrete reconstruction of the Edo-period original, but it sits within an extensive park of original moats, stone walls, and gate structures that convey the castle's genuine scale. The keep museum documents the Hojo clan's extraordinary 100-year dominance of eastern Japan in considerable detail. In late March the castle park's 300 cherry trees make it one of the finest blossom spots in the greater Tokyo region.

💡 The castle park is freely accessible; the keep interior charges ¥510. Arrive early in cherry blossom season — the park fills completely by late morning on peak weekends. The outer moat, the Sumiyoshi-bashi bridge, and the Higashi-Akagane-mon gate are all original or carefully restored structures worth examining closely.

Ninomaru Garden

二の丸広場 · 本丸庭園

Within the castle's second bailey, the Ninomaru Garden is a traditional Japanese stroll garden of considerable beauty — iris beds, a central pond, stone lanterns, and old pine trees arranged around a garden that originally served the Edo-period castle lord's inner compound. In June the irises are spectacular; in April the plum and cherry extend the castle park's blossom season beyond the main cherry trees. The garden is frequently overlooked by visitors focused on the keep, making it one of the quieter and more pleasant spots within the castle grounds. The restored Tokiwagi-mon gate at the garden's north entrance is one of the finest surviving Edo-period gate structures in Kanagawa.

💡 The iris garden peaks in mid-June and is one of the best in the Kanto region at that time — a specific reason to visit Odawara outside cherry blossom season. The Odawara Samurai Museum adjacent to the castle grounds has good interactive exhibits on samurai equipment and the Hojo clan.

The Old Tokaido

東海道 · 小田原宿

Odawara was the 9th of the 53 post stations on the old Tokaido highway between Edo and Kyoto, and the town's merchant quarter along the old highway route retains more of its Edo-period character than most surviving post towns in the Kanto region. The street running north from the castle toward the station follows the original Tokaido alignment, and along it a number of old merchant houses, some converted into shops and restaurants, still show the deep eaves, thick plaster walls, and latticed fronts of Edo-period commercial architecture. Several of Odawara's famous kamaboko (fish cake) shops have been trading on this street for generations.

💡 The Odawara-juku Honjin Kinenkan near the station marks the site of the official post station inn where daimyo and officials rested on the highway — a small but well-presented museum explaining the Tokaido's significance. The walk from the castle to the station along the old highway takes about 20 minutes and passes the best concentration of historic buildings.

Odawara Fish Market

小田原漁港 · 魚市場

Odawara's fishing port on the coast below the castle has supplied the city — and Tokyo — with seafood from Sagami Bay for centuries. The Odawara Uoichiba (fish market complex) near the port is open to the general public and houses a dozen seafood restaurants and stalls offering the day's catch in sashimi sets, grilled fish, and the various kamaboko and himono (dried fish) products that Odawara is famous for producing. The fishing boats work the bay for aji (horse mackerel), saba (mackerel), and sakura ebi (cherry shrimp) that appear on the restaurant menus according to season. It's one of the most accessible and enjoyable fish market lunch experiences within reach of Tokyo.

💡 The Odawara Uoichiba restaurant complex is busiest at lunchtime — arrive by 11:30 AM for the best selection and shortest queues. The aji (horse mackerel) dishes are the local speciality — aji sashimi, aji fry, and aji-no-himono (dried mackerel) are all worth trying. The port itself is a 15-minute walk or short bus ride from the castle.

Kamaboko — The Fish Cake Tradition

蒲鉾 · 小田原名産

Odawara has been producing kamaboko — steamed fish cake, a staple of Japanese cuisine — since the Edo period, when the abundant fish of Sagami Bay and the town's position on the Tokaido made it the natural place to perfect and distribute the product. Today the city has over 70 kamaboko producers, ranging from centuries-old traditional shops to modern factory outlets, and the craft has been designated an Odawara traditional industry. The shops along the old Tokaido and near the castle offer tastings, workshops where you can make your own kamaboko, and a range of products that show how far beyond simple fish cake the tradition extends — soufflés, grilled slabs, decorative shapes, and seasonal varieties.

💡 Suzuhiro Kamaboko-no-Sato — a kamaboko museum and workshop complex — offers hands-on making sessions (book in advance), a restaurant, and an extensive shop about 10 minutes from the station by bus. The simpler option is to buy directly from the old shops along the Tokaido and eat while walking. Kamaboko keeps well and makes an excellent omiyage (souvenir) to take back to Tokyo.

The Coastal Promenade

海岸散歩道 · 相模湾

Odawara's coast faces southwest across Sagami Bay toward the Izu Peninsula, and on clear afternoons the views from the coastal promenade extend across the bay to the mountains of Izu with Mount Fuji appearing above them on the best days. The beach itself is a working coast of grey pebbles rather than a resort beach, which keeps it quiet and genuine — local families, fishermen checking their gear, the occasional surfer. The walk along the promenade from the port back toward the station, with the castle visible on the hill above and the bay stretching south, is one of the most pleasant end-of-day walks in the greater Tokyo day-trip circuit.

💡 The best Fuji views from the coast are in winter and early spring when the air is clearest — on a good day in January the mountain appears enormous above the bay. The coastal promenade is about 1.5 km from end to end and connects the port to the Odawara beach area; allow 30–40 minutes for a comfortable walk before catching the train back to Tokyo.
