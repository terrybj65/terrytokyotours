Shinjuku

Gardens, neon, memory lanes & the night.

East & West · Shinjuku · Tokyo Metropolitan Government

Shinjuku contains multitudes. The world's busiest train station sits at the centre of a district that manages to be simultaneously a major business hub, a vast entertainment quarter, one of Tokyo's finest gardens, a postwar memory lane of yakitori smoke and cheap whisky, and a warren of tiny bars where the 20th century never quite ended. It rewards both the first-time visitor and the person who has been coming for decades — there is always another alley, another door, another version of the city behind the one you know.

Pulse of the City
Streets & Energy · East Exit

East Exit Plaza & Yasukuni-dori

東口広場 · 靖国通り

The East Exit of Shinjuku Station opens onto a perpetually busy plaza that is both a crossroads and a spectacle — the meeting point of commuters, tourists, street performers, and the full human spectrum of one of the world's largest cities. Yasukuni-dori running east from the exit is lined with electronics shops, music stores, pachinko parlours, and department stores that merge into one another without pause. The area immediately east of the station — Studio Alta and its giant screen, the covered Shinjuku Marui department store arcade, the small camera shops — is best experienced on foot, slowly, with no particular destination.

💡 The large Studio Alta screen above the east exit plaza has been one of Tokyo's famous meeting points since the 1980s — "meet you under Alta" is still how locals arrange to find each other in Shinjuku. The underground shopping arcades below the plaza connect east and west without surfacing — useful to know in rain.

Tokyo's Best Bar District
Bars & Night Culture · Kabukicho Area

Golden Gai

ゴールデン街

Golden Gai is a six-lane network of narrow alleys containing approximately 200 tiny bars, most with capacity for between 5 and 12 people. The bars date from the 1950s and 60s and the buildings have been repeatedly threatened with demolition — saved each time by a combination of local resistance and the sheer improbability of replacing something so specific and irreplaceable. Each bar has its own character: some cater to writers, some to musicians, some to film directors, some to foreign visitors, some to regulars who have been coming for forty years. Walking the alleys, looking at the handwritten signs and faded photographs taped to windows, and choosing a door to open is one of the finest things you can do in Tokyo at night.

💡 Most bars charge a small table fee (¥500–1,000) that covers snacks or a drink — check before sitting. Some bars are regulars-only, marked with signs; if in doubt, peek in and ask. Wander the alleys first before choosing — the variety is half the pleasure.

The Neon Quarter
Entertainment District · Northeast of Station

Kabukicho

歌舞伎町

Kabukicho is Tokyo's most famous entertainment district — a dense grid of neon, host clubs, cinemas, game centres, karaoke buildings, and restaurants that operates at maximum intensity from early evening until well past dawn. It was named after a kabuki theatre that was planned but never built after the war, and it has been the city's pleasure district ever since. The main boulevard, Ichibangai, runs north from the east exit under an arch of neon signs; on either side the streets become progressively more intense. The new Kabukicho Tower — a 48-storey entertainment and hotel complex that opened in 2023 — now anchors the north end of the district and contains theatres, restaurants, and a hotel with direct views over the neon grid below.

💡 Kabukicho is safe to walk during both day and night, but keep your wits about you and decline approaches from touts on the street. The south end of the district near the Don Quijote store has the best concentration of late-night ramen shops for a post-Golden Gai meal. The Toho Cinemas in the new tower shows both Japanese and international films.

Memory Lane
Food & History · West Exit

Omoide Yokocho

思い出横丁

Immediately west of Shinjuku Station, Omoide Yokocho — Memory Lane — is a narrow alley of tiny yakitori restaurants and drinking stalls that has existed since the postwar black market of the late 1940s. The lane is barely wide enough for two people to pass, the ceilings are low, the ventilation is non-existent, and the smoke from the charcoal grills fills everything with the smell of charred chicken skin and scallions. Most restaurants have fewer than ten seats; some have four. The menu at each is similar — chicken skewers, cold beer, cheap whisky highballs — and the experience is as close as you can get in modern Tokyo to the city as it was 70 years ago.

💡 Arrive at 5–6 PM before the seats fill completely. Just walk in to whichever restaurant has a gap. Don't overthink it — they're all roughly the same quality and the experience is about the atmosphere, not a particular restaurant. Order the negima (chicken and spring onion) skewers and a draft beer first.

Shinjuku Gyoen

新宿御苑

Shinjuku Gyoen is Tokyo's finest public garden and one of the great urban parks of Asia — 58 hectares of immaculately maintained landscape that blends a formal French garden, an English landscape garden, and a traditional Japanese garden into a single coherent whole. The grounds were originally an imperial garden and retain that sense of deliberate, unhurried beauty. On weekday mornings it's nearly empty, and the combination of old-growth plane trees, greenhouse-warm conservatories, and carefully raked gravel gives it the feeling of an entirely separate city within the city. In late March the cherry blossoms here are unmatched in central Tokyo.

💡 Enter via the Shinjuku Gate (closest to the station) for the formal French garden first, then work east toward the Japanese garden and pond. The greenhouse is often overlooked — it houses an extraordinary tropical and desert plant collection and is worth 30 minutes on its own. Entry is ¥500.

Department Store Culture
Shopping & Food · South Exit

Takashimaya Times Square

タカシマヤ タイムズスクエア

Directly connected to Shinjuku Station's south exit, Takashimaya Times Square is one of the great Japanese department stores — 15 floors of curated retail, restaurants, and food halls that represent the Japanese depato at its most complete. The basement food halls (depachika) alone are worth an extended visit: two floors of prepared foods, patisseries, sake specialists, wagashi sweets, bento counters, and fresh produce from across Japan, presented with the kind of care that makes grocery shopping feel like a cultural activity. The upper floors transition from fashion through homeware to a rooftop garden. The restaurant floors (12–14F) cover Japanese, French, Chinese, and everything in between.

💡 The basement food halls get extremely busy at lunchtime and after 5 PM — visit mid-afternoon for the best experience. The Tokyu Hands store attached to the building is several floors of inspired Japanese stationery, tools, and lifestyle goods and can absorb an hour without difficulty.
