Asia's largest Chinatown · Port city

When Japan opened to the world in 1859, Yokohama was the port it chose. Within a generation, the city had grown into one of Asia's most cosmopolitan harbours — and it still feels that way. Today Yokohama is a city of red lanterns and steaming dumplings, Victorian warehouses turned into galleries and restaurants, sweeping waterfront promenades, and a hilltop neighbourhood of roses and foreign consulates. Just half an hour from Tokyo, yet completely its own world.

The Yokohama Itinerary
01
City Icon
Waterfront District · Minato Mirai 21

Minato Mirai 21

みなとみらい21

Yokohama's showpiece waterfront district was built on reclaimed land in the 1980s and 90s, and unlike most urban renewal projects, it actually worked. The Landmark Tower — Japan's second-tallest building — anchors one end; the restored red-brick Yokohama Port Opening Commemorative Hall anchors the other. Between them: the Cosmo World ferris wheel, the Yokohama Museum of Art, the Pacifico convention centre, and a network of elevated boardwalks that let you trace the harbour edge for nearly two kilometres. At night the entire district reflects across the water in streaks of blue and white light — one of the better urban night views in Japan.

💡 Take the elevator to the Landmark Tower Sky Garden observation deck (69th floor) on a clear day for views stretching to Mount Fuji. The Yokohama Red Brick Warehouse nearby is worth a walk-through for its craft shops and harbour terrace café.

02
End of Day
Cultural Venue · Minato Mirai

Red Brick Warehouse

横浜赤レンガ倉庫

End the day where the city's history meets its present. The two Meiji-era customs warehouses on the harbour edge were built between 1911 and 1913 and have been repurposed into a cultural venue with craft breweries, independent boutiques, art exhibitions, and one of the best outdoor terraces in the city. As the sun drops behind Minato Mirai and the ferris wheel starts to glow, the waterfront terrace in front of the warehouses fills with the particular ease of a city that knows how to exhale. There are worse places to have a final beer and watch the harbour lights come on before catching the train back to Tokyo.

💡 The open-air terrace faces west — perfect for sunset. The Yokohama Craft Beer festival runs here several times a year, with dozens of small Japanese breweries pouring. Check the schedule before you visit.

03
Hidden Gem
Historic Garden · Honmoku

Sankeien Garden

三渓園

A short bus ride from the waterfront, Sankeien is Yokohama's great secret — a sprawling traditional garden assembled by silk merchant Hara Sankei in the early 20th century from historic structures transplanted from Kyoto, Kamakura, and the Kinki region. Farmhouses, tea houses, temples, and a three-storey pagoda are scattered across 17.5 hectares of ponds, bamboo groves, and plum orchards. The inner garden, where the oldest structures are concentrated, is unhurried and genuinely beautiful in all seasons. Most visitors to Yokohama never come here, which makes it one of the most peaceful places in the entire city.

💡 Combine a morning at the waterfront with an afternoon at Sankeien. The plum blossoms in February, irises in June, and autumn colour in November are each spectacular. Allow at least 90 minutes to walk the full garden.

Waterfront
Park & Promenade · Waterfront

Yamashita Park

山下公園

Running along the harbour edge directly south of Chinatown, Yamashita Park is Yokohama's great breathing space — a long, manicured promenade with flower beds, fountains, and uninterrupted views across the port. The retired ocean liner Hikawa Maru sits moored at the park's edge and can be toured inside; built in 1929, it once carried passengers between Yokohama and Seattle before becoming a floating museum. On clear days the Minato Mirai skyline and the distant hills of Kanagawa frame the view perfectly. This is the place to decompress between the sensory intensity of Chinatown and the rest of the day.

💡 The park is lovely in any season — cherry blossoms in spring, rose blooms in May, lantern festivals in summer. The harbour cruise boats depart from the pier at the park's eastern end if you want to see the skyline from the water.

Historic Quarter
Historic District · Yamate

Yamate — The Bluff

山手

When the port opened in 1859, foreign traders and diplomats were assigned to the hillside above the harbour — a district they called the Bluff. They planted rose gardens, built Victorian mansions and consulates, and laid out tree-lined streets that feel unlike anywhere else in Japan. Many of the original Western-style homes survive and are open to visitors: the Bluff 18 Residence, the Yamate Museum, the British House, the French consulate garden. Walking the main street, Yamate Honchō, through the dappled shade of old plane trees with glimpses of the port below is one of the most pleasant things you can do in Yokohama on a mild afternoon.

💡 The rose garden at the Harbour View Park (Minato-no-Mieru-Oka Koen) peaks in May and October. The park also has the best free elevated view of Minato Mirai and the bay — bring a picnic and stay for the late afternoon light.

Food & Culture · Chūkagai

Yokohama Chinatown

横浜中華街

Asia's largest Chinatown — and by most measures the most spectacular — covers over half a square kilometre of Yokohama's downtown, with more than 600 restaurants, shops, and temples packed inside ten ornate gates. The main street, Chūkagai-dōri, runs under a canopy of red lanterns past roast duck windows and steamer carts piled high with xiaolongbao. The neighbourhood has been here since the 1860s, when Chinese merchants followed the early traders into the port, and the cooking reflects that deep rootedness — Cantonese, Shanghainese, and Sichuan traditions sit alongside one another on the same narrow block.

💡 Arrive hungry and walk the whole perimeter before choosing where to eat. The best dim sum restaurants often have queues by 11 AM. Look for the Kantei-byo temple in the middle of the quarter — a working Taoist shrine draped in incense smoke, free to enter, and easy to miss.
