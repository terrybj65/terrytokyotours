Hakone

Hot springs, volcanic peaks & Fuji on the horizon.

Explore stops ↓ Book a tour
Gora · Hakone-Yumoto · Miyanoshita · Moto-Hakone · Owakudani · Togendai

Hakone is where Tokyo goes when it needs to breathe. Ninety minutes from Shinjuku by the Romancecar express, the national park wraps a chain of volcanic mountains, hot spring valleys, a crater still venting sulphurous steam, and a vast caldera lake around one of the most celebrated views of Mount Fuji on earth. The classic Hakone route is a loop — mountain railway, cable car, ropeway, lake ferry, and mountain highway — that connects seven forms of transport into one continuous journey through landscape that shifts from cedar forest to open volcanic hillside to the lake's edge, with Fuji appearing and disappearing through the cloud as it pleases.

Into the Mountains
Cable Car & Park · Gora

Gora & the Cable Car

強羅 · ケーブルカー

Gora is the mountain railway's upper terminus and the transfer point for the cable car ascending to Sounzan, where the ropeway begins. The town itself is pleasant — a spread of ryokan and small restaurants on the steep hillside with a well-maintained public park, the Hakone Museum of Art, and the Gora Kadan, one of Japan's most celebrated traditional ryokan. The cable car from Gora to Sounzan takes about 10 minutes and rises through dense forest before emerging at the ropeway terminal. The Hakone Open-Air Museum, a ten-minute walk from Gora or one stop on the mountain railway at Chokoku-no-Mori, is one of Japan's finest outdoor sculpture parks and merits a full visit if time allows.

💡 The Hakone Open-Air Museum (one stop before Gora at Chokoku-no-Mori) has a superb collection of Picasso ceramics and graphics alongside major outdoor sculptures by Moore, Rodin, and Giacometti — set across a hillside with Fuji visible on clear days. Allow 90 minutes and factor it into the day's timing. Entry is covered by some Hakone passes.

🚞 Hakone Tozan Railway
02
Steepest Railway in Japan
Mountain Railway · Yumoto to Gora

Hakone Tozan Railway

箱根登山鉄道

The Hakone Tozan Railway is one of Japan's most celebrated mountain railways — a narrow-gauge line climbing 445 metres from Hakone-Yumoto to Gora through steep cedar forest, crossing its own tracks three times via switchback turns that allow it to gain altitude on gradients too steep for a conventional railway. The journey takes 40 minutes and is best experienced in a window seat on the left side heading up. In June and July the lineside hydrangeas — tens of thousands of them, planted along the entire route — come into full bloom and the railway runs special late evening services so passengers can view the illuminated flowers in the dark. At other seasons the autumn maples and winter snow are equally beautiful.

💡 Board at Hakone-Yumoto and sit on the left side (window seat) heading toward Gora for the best views of the valley below. The Miyanoshita stop midway is worth a brief detour for the historic Fujiya Hotel — Japan's oldest Western-style resort hotel, opened in 1878, with a lobby worth seeing even if you're not staying.

03
Gateway to Hakone
Hot Spring Town · Arrival Point

Hakone-Yumoto

箱根湯本

The gateway to Hakone and the terminus of the Odakyu Romancecar from Shinjuku, Hakone-Yumoto is a classic Japanese hot spring town strung along the Hayakawa River — a compact strip of ryokan, souvenir shops, onsen day-bath facilities, and small restaurants that has been welcoming travellers from Tokyo since the Edo period when the town sat on the old Tokaido highway. The river itself, flanked by cedar and maple, is a pleasure to walk along, and the town has enough charm to justify an hour before boarding the mountain railway. The Tenzan Tohji-kyo outdoor bath complex up the valley road is one of the best value day-use onsen in Hakone.

💡 Pick up your Hakone Free Pass from the Odakyu kiosk at Hakone-Yumoto Station if you didn't get it at Shinjuku — it covers everything from here on. Leave heavy luggage at the station coin lockers and travel light for the loop. The Minatoya shop near the station is the place for Hakone's famous Yumoto manju sweet bean cakes.

⛵ Lake Ferry · Togendai to Moto-Hakone
04
The Fuji View
Caldera Lake · Togendai Pier

Lake Ashi

芦ノ湖

The ropeway descends from Owakudani to Togendai on the northern shore of Lake Ashi — a vast caldera lake formed in the same volcanic event as Owakudani, 11 km in circumference and 43 metres at its deepest. From Togendai pier, replica pirate galleons operated by Izuhakone Steamship Company cross the lake to Hakone-machi and Moto-Hakone in about 40 minutes — one of the most scenic ferry crossings in Japan. On clear mornings, Mount Fuji is reflected in the lake's surface to the southwest, giving rise to one of Japan's most iconic and reproduced views. The Hakone Shrine torii gate standing in the lake at Moto-Hakone, visible from the ferry's approach, is the other defining image of the area.

💡 The best Fuji views from the lake are from the western and southern shores — sit on the starboard (right) side of the ferry heading from Togendai to Moto-Hakone for the mountain to be in view. Early morning and winter days give the clearest sightlines; by afternoon clouds often obscure the summit. The pirate galleons are kitsch but genuinely fun.

End of the Loop
Shrine & Old Highway · South Shore

Moto-Hakone & Hakone Shrine

元箱根 · 箱根神社

The southern shore of Lake Ashi is Hakone's most historically layered area. Hakone Shrine, founded in 757 AD and approached through a cedar avenue that runs for over a kilometre from the lake shore up into the forest, is one of the most atmospheric shrines in the Kanto region — its famous red torii gate standing in the shallows of the lake is the image that defines Hakone internationally. Nearby, a preserved section of the old Tokaido highway runs under 400-year-old cedar trees — the same road that Edo-period travellers walked between Tokyo and Kyoto. The village of Moto-Hakone at the lake's edge has good restaurants for a final meal before the bus back to Hakone-Yumoto and the Romancecar home.

💡 Walk the full cedar avenue to Hakone Shrine — about 20 minutes from the lake — rather than taking the shortcut. The avenue, planted with trees that have stood for four centuries, is one of the finest walks in the national park. The lake torii is accessible on foot from the shrine's lower gate; arrive early morning for the mist-on-water effect that makes the photographs look like paintings.

🚠 Hakone Ropeway
06
Volcanic Valley
Volcanic Crater · Ropeway Stop

Owakudani

大涌谷 · Great Boiling Valley

Owakudani — the Great Boiling Valley — is the most dramatic stop on the Hakone loop: a still-active volcanic crater formed 3,000 years ago when Mount Hakone last erupted, its vents still releasing sulphurous steam and boiling water that has been used to cook eggs since the Edo period. The ropeway passes directly over the crater on its way between Sounzan and Togendai, giving a bird's-eye view of the steaming vents and ashen ground below. At the Owakudani station, a short (sometimes closed) walking trail leads into the crater area where the famous kuro-tamago — eggs hard-boiled black in the sulphurous spring water — are sold. On clear days Mount Fuji is framed perfectly to the west.

💡 Kuro-tamago (black eggs) are sold in bags of five for around ¥600 — legend says each egg extends your life by seven years, making a bag of five worth 35 extra years. The Owakudani station has the best accessible Fuji view on the entire loop; if the mountain is visible, spend time here before continuing down to the lake. Note the crater walking trail is sometimes closed due to volcanic activity — check before visiting.
